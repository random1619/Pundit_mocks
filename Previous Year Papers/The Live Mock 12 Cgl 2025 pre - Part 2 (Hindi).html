    Who discovered the nucleus of the cell?

    Watson

    Robert Hook

    Anton van Leeuwenhoek    

    Robert Brown

Solution

Robert Brown, a Scottish botanist, discovered the nucleus of the cell in 1831. While studying plant cells, he observed a distinct, rounded structure within the cells, which he named the "nucleus." This discovery was a significant milestone in cell biology.


The discovery of the cell is credited to Robert Hooke in 1665. Using a compound microscope, he observed a thin slice of cork and noticed small, box-like structures, which he called "cells" because they resembled the cells of a monastery. This marked the first identification of cells, though Hooke was observing dead plant cells.


Later, Anton van Leeuwenhoek advanced microscopy and became the first to observe living cells, including bacteria and protozoa, which he referred to as "animalcules."
These discoveries laid the foundation for cell theory, which was further developed by scientists like Matthias Schleiden, Theodor Schwann, and Rudolf Virchow.

    Ebony and Mahogany trees are found in which type of forests?

    Montane forests

    Tropical evergreen forests

    Tropical deciduous forests    

    Mangrove forests

Solution
Ebony and Mahogany trees are typically found in tropical evergreen forests. These forests are characterized by dense, multi-layered vegetation and remain green throughout the year due to consistent rainfall and warm temperatures. Such forests are common in regions near the equator.
There are several main types of forests in India, including:
1. Tropical Evergreen Forests: Found in regions with heavy rainfall, like the Western Ghats, Andaman and Nicobar Islands, and parts of the north-eastern states. Characterized by dense, lush greenery throughout the year. Key tree species: Ebony, Mahogany, Rosewood.
2. Tropical Deciduous Forests: Also known as monsoon forests, they shed their leaves during dry seasons to conserve water. Found across central India, the Deccan plateau, and the foothills of the Himalayas. Key tree species: Teak, Sal, Sandalwood.
3. Tropical Thorn Forests: Found in arid and semi-arid regions like Rajasthan, Gujarat, and parts of Punjab and Haryana. These forests have drought-resistant plants and scattered vegetation. Key species: Acacias, Euphorbias, Cacti.
4. Montane Forests: Found in mountainous regions like the Himalayas. Vegetation varies with altitude, ranging from tropical forests in lower altitudes to coniferous forests at higher altitudes. Key tree species: Pine, Deodar, Fir.
5. Mangrove Forests: Found in coastal regions and tidal areas, such as the Sunderbans in West Bengal and parts of Odisha and Andhra Pradesh. Adapted to saline and brackish water conditions. Key species: Sundari trees.
6. Littoral and Swamp Forests: Located along riverbanks, marshlands, and deltas. These forests are waterlogged and rich in biodiversity.
Each forest type plays a critical role in sustaining wildlife, regulating the climate, and supporting human livelihoods.
