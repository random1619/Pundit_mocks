If inflation is to be combated, the RBI:

Raises SLR and lowers CRR

Lowers SLR and raises CRR

Raises both SLR and CRR    

Lowers both SLR and CRR

Solution

Rise in CRR:


a) Increase in CRR: Banks are required to hold a higher percentage of their deposits as reserves with the central bank.
b) Increased cash reserves for a given amount of demand deposits: Banks must set aside more money from their deposits, reducing the funds available for lending.
c) Decrease in money supply of commercial banks: Due to reduced lending capacity caused by the higher CRR, the overall money supply in the economy decreases.
d) Inflation is controlled: This reduction in money supply helps to control inflationary pressures.


Rise in SLR:


a) Increase in SLR: Banks are mandated to hold a higher percentage of their net demand and time liabilities as liquid assets.
b) Increased liquid assets held by commercial banks: Banks need to increase their holdings of liquid assets, which are not available for lending.
c) Decrease in availability of funds for CRR deposits with the RBI: With more funds tied up in liquid assets, banks have less money available for deposits required under CRR.
d) Decrease in money supply of commercial banks: Similar to the effect of an increase in CRR, the higher SLR reduces the funds available for lending, thereby reducing the money supply.
e) Inflation is controlled: By limiting lending capacity and reducing available funds for investment, SLR helps control inflationary pressures.

Gulf Stream

California Current    

Florida Current

Solution

Warm Currents: These flow from equatorial regions towards the poles. They tend to have higher surface temperatures because they originate in warmer regions and transport heat towards higher latitudes. Examples include the Gulf Stream in the North Atlantic and the Kuroshio Current in the North Pacific.
